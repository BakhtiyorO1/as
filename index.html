Geographical Situation:

Samarkand is located in the southeastern part of Uzbekistan, in the fertile Zarafshan River valley. It is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in Central Asia and lies along the historic Silk Road trading route. The city is situated approximately 275 km southwest of Tashkent, the capital of Uzbekistan, and 75 km west of the border with Tajikistan.

Coordinates:

Latitude: 39° 39′ N

Longitude: 66° 58′ E

Altitude:

Approximately 700 meters (2,300 feet) above sea level

Climate:

Samarkand experiences a continental climate with hot summers and cold winters. Summers can reach temperatures above 40°C (104°F), while winters often drop below freezing.

Nature:

Samarkand is located in a region where the natural landscape offers a striking mix of mountain scenery, fertile valleys, and desert plains. The city lies in the Zarafshan River valley, surrounded by the Nuratau Mountains to the north and the arid Kyzylkum Desert to the south and west. This diverse environment creates a balance between lush green oases and dry, rocky landscapes.

The climate in Samarkand is sharply continental, with hot, dry summers and cold winters. Seasonal changes bring dramatic contrasts in temperature, shaping the lifestyle and agriculture of the area. The natural setting supports vineyards, orchards, and gardens that flourish around the city, fed by the waters of the Zarafshan River and local irrigation canals.

Rivers:

The main water source in the Samarkand region is the Zarafshan River, which originates in the mountains of Tajikistan. It flows through Samarkand, providing vital irrigation for agriculture and fresh water to the city.

The Zarafshan River runs about 781 kilometers, passing through fertile valleys and eventually disappearing into the desert before reaching the Sarykamysh Lake basin. Its name means “spreader of gold,” reflecting the historical importance of the river for local settlements and trade routes.

Samarkand’s reliance on the Zarafshan and its irrigation systems demonstrates the age-old importance of water management in this arid environment, sustaining both the natural landscape and the rich cultural heritage of the city.

Lakes:

Although Samarkand is not known for large natural lakes, the region benefits from nearby water reservoirs and man-made bodies of water. One of the most notable is the Tudakul Reservoir, located between Samarkand and Bukhara, which is used for irrigation, fishing, and seasonal recreation.

Natural lakes are rare in this part of Uzbekistan, as the landscape is more dominated by mountains, river valleys, and deserts. However, small ponds and artificial lakes are created around country resorts and recreation zones, especially in rural areas of the Samarkand region, for local use and agricultural needs.

While not directly in Samarkand, Aydarkul Lake, located in the Kyzylkum Desert, is reachable by long drive and offers a peaceful desert retreat, where visitors can enjoy birdwatching, swimming, and yurt stays far from the busy city life.

Flora:

The flora of the Samarkand region is shaped by its diverse landscape—mountain foothills, semi-desert plains, and fertile river valleys. In the Zarafshan Valley, surrounding Samarkand, a variety of plant life thrives due to irrigation from the Zarafshan River. These include orchards, vineyards, grassy meadows, and flowering plants.

In the foothills and low mountains near Samarkand, you can find wild-growing species such as almonds, wild cherries, hawthorn, and juniper trees, particularly in areas with natural springs and streams. The region is also rich in seasonal wildflowers like tulips, irises, and poppies during spring.

The steppe and semi-desert areas surrounding the region feature drought-resistant shrubs and bushes, some of which are endemic to Central Asia. These plants play a vital role in preserving soil and supporting local wildlife.

Samarkand's blend of cultivated greenery in the city and wild flora in the surrounding areas makes it a unique natural environment shaped by both nature and human care over centuries.

People:

Samarkand is one of the most culturally diverse cities in Uzbekistan. While Uzbeks make up the majority of the population, the city is also home to a significant number of Tajiks, as well as smaller communities of Russians, Tatars, and other nationalities. This ethnic diversity is rooted in Samarkand’s rich historical past as a crossroads of civilizations along the Great Silk Road.

Samarkand’s population is known for its hospitality, strong family values, and deep respect for cultural traditions. The city is a vibrant blend of ancient heritage and modern urban life, reflected in the customs, cuisine, and celebrations of its residents.

Ethnogeny:

The population of Samarkand reflects the broader ethnogenetic background of Uzbekistan. The Uzbeks of this region, like elsewhere, have Turkic roots with a blend of Caucasian and Mongoloid features due to centuries of migrations and conquests. Ancient peoples such as the Sogdians, Bactrians, and Scythians once inhabited this land, contributing to the rich genetic and cultural mix of today’s inhabitants.

The Tajik population in Samarkand has historically been substantial, as the region was once a major center of Persian culture and language. This coexistence has created a unique cultural and ethnic mosaic in the city.

Language:

The official language in Samarkand is Uzbek, which belongs to the Turkic language family. It is used in education, government, and public life. However, due to the historical and ethnic presence of Tajiks, Tajik (a dialect of Persian) is also widely spoken, especially in families, daily communication, and among the older generation.

Russian is also commonly used, particularly in business, education, and among mixed-ethnic communities. This multilingual environment adds to the cultural richness of Samarkand and reflects its status as one of Uzbekistan’s oldest and most historically significant cities.

Religion:

Samarkand, like the rest of Uzbekistan, is part of a secular state where different religious communities coexist peacefully. The city is home to a variety of religious organizations, including mosques, Orthodox churches, and Jewish synagogues.

The vast majority of Samarkand’s population—about 93%—are Muslims, predominantly Sunni of the Hanafi school of thought. Islam plays a significant role in the cultural and social life of the city, with many important religious sites and historic mosques attracting both worshippers and tourists.

Christianity is practiced by a smaller percentage of the population, mainly Orthodox Christians (around 4%), with Catholics and other groups making up about 3%. Religious tolerance is a strong value in Samarkand, and freedom of religion is guaranteed by the Constitution of Uzbekistan, allowing all residents to freely follow or not follow any religion.

Public Holidays:

Residents of Samarkand celebrate the same official holidays as the rest of Uzbekistan. These include:

New Year’s Day (January 1) — marking the start of the calendar year, celebrated nationwide.

Day of Defenders of the Motherland (January 14) — commemorating the establishment of Uzbekistan’s Armed Forces.

International Women’s Day (March 8) — honoring women and girls across the country.

Navruz (March 21) — the Persian New Year, celebrated with traditional festivities welcoming spring.

Remembrance Day (May 9) — honoring those who sacrificed their lives during World War II in the fight against fascism.

Independence Day (September 1) — celebrating Uzbekistan’s independence and national pride.

Teachers’ Day (October 1) — recognizing the contribution of teachers and trainers.

Constitution Day (December 8) — marking the adoption of the country’s Constitution.

Money:

The national currency used in Samarkand, as throughout Uzbekistan, is the Uzbek sum (UZS). This currency has been in circulation since 1994 and is the only legal means of payment in the city. Banknotes come in denominations of 200, 500, 1,000, 5,000, 10,000, 50,000, and 100,000 sum, while coins are available in 200 and 500 sum.

Each banknote features different colors and showcases important cultural and historical monuments of Uzbekistan, some of which are landmarks in Samarkand itself, reflecting the city’s rich heritage. Although smaller denominations like 100 sum bills and some coins are no longer in everyday use, the larger notes and coins remain common.

Foreign currencies such as US dollars, euros, British pounds sterling, and Japanese yen can be exchanged easily at banks, exchange offices, and many hotels in Samarkand.

Electricity:

Electricity in Samarkand, as in the rest of Uzbekistan, operates on a 220 Volt, 50 Hertz system. The power plugs are round two-pin types (type C or F), so travelers are advised to bring suitable adapters if their devices have different plugs.
